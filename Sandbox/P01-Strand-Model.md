In Christoph Schiller's strand model, particles are not point-like entities but are described as rational (unknotted) tangles of fluctuating strands. This model aims to unify physics by deriving the Standard Model and general relativity from a single principle: the motion of strands with Planck-scale radius. The strand model accounts for the exact spectrum of particles found in the Standard Model—no more and no less—and explicitly predicts the absence of any additional elementary particles beyond these. Fundamental Classification of Particles in the Strand Model The model classifies particles based on their tangle topology and the way they move through space (which itself is a network of blurred crisscrossing strands): Fermions (Matter Particles): These are described as rational tangles that move via the "belt trick." This mechanism explains why they have half-integer spin and why their wave function phase rotates as the tangle core advances.Quarks: Derived from specific tangle topologies that carry color charge and participate in strong interactions.Leptons: Tangle topologies corresponding to the electron, muon, tau, and their respective neutrinos.Bosons (Force Carriers): These result from the deformations of tangles or specific strand configurations.Photons: Described as twisted strands that are untangled, which explains why they are massless and move at the maximum speed \(c\).Gluons, W, and Z Bosons: These correspond to the three known gauge groups (SU(3), SU(2), and U(1)), which the model derives from the three Reidemeister moves of strand tangles.Higgs Boson: Described as a specific braid that leads to Yukawa terms only for tangles of two or three strands (explaining why only certain particles gain mass).Gravitons: While the Standard Model does not include them, the strand model describes gravitons as microscopic fluctuations of the strands that make up space. The Full Particle Spectrum According to the strand model, all known elementary particles are represented by unique tangle configurations.
