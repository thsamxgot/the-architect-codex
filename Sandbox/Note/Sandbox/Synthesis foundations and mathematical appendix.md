## Part III: Mathematical Appendix

### A. Modified Gauss Law Derivation

In \(D = 3 + \varepsilon\) effective dimensions, the flux of the gravitational field through a hypersphere of radius \(r\) scales as:

\[
\oint g \cdot dA \propto r^{D-1} g(r).
\]

Conservation of flux implies:

\[
r^{2+\varepsilon} g(r) = G M.
\]

Thus:

\[
g(r) = \frac{G M}{r^{2+\varepsilon}}.
\]

For small \(\varepsilon\), this can be written as:

\[
g(r) \approx \frac{G M}{r^2} \left(1 + \varepsilon \ln r \right).
\]

This logarithmic correction produces enhanced gravity at galactic scales.

---

### B. Rotation Curve Implication

Orbital velocity satisfies:

\[
v^2(r) = r g(r).
\]

Substituting:

\[
v^2(r) \approx \frac{G M}{r} \left(1 + \varepsilon \ln r \right).
\]

At large \(r\), the logarithmic term dominates, yielding asymptotically flat rotation curves.

---

### C. Newtonian Recovery

For high-density or small-radius systems:

\[
\varepsilon \ln r \ll 1,
\]

and standard Newtonian dynamics is recovered to leading order.

---

### D. Cosmological Friedmann Equation

In a homogeneous universe, the Codex-modified Friedmann equation reads:

\[
H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3} (\rho_m + \alpha \, \rho_s).
\]

With \(\alpha = \varepsilon\), the late-time dominance of the space-tension term yields accelerated expansion without a cosmological constant.
